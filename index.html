Letter from the Editors

Welcome to the Observer’s Literary Issue. This spring, it is our pleasure to bring you 22 pieces of poetry & prose from the Tufts community.

These pieces are about home, family, linguistics, knitting, sports, translation, birth, religion, and land. They are phone calls to a friend. They write across the lines of what is legal and not legal. They lay bare events and experiences that often go undocumented. Richard White writes, “there are regions of the past that only memory knows,” and these poems are maps of those regions.

Why do we devote an entire issue to poetry and prose? The work inside these pages does what the Observer does in every issue. These pieces are news, opinions, arts, campus life, and personal narratives.

There are things that are easy to say in essays or in articles or behind podiums—charismatic arguments, slogans, and sweeping claims about the state of the ____. These poems contain truths that do not fit neatly into a single slogan or claim. They hold multiple voices and ideas at once, often in the same line or word.

We love these pieces because they expose the quotidian that lives within the stories that end up on the news or in our class readings. They are about what it feels like to live inside history, when looking back means looking around, forward and away.

These writers take the world—its snack foods, its canyons and tourist-filled beaches, its terrifying histories—and rearrange it to show us the possibilities of what this world can be, or maybe already is. We are grateful to the writers for giving us their words. Thank you for giving us the specific joy of making this year’s Literary Issue.

Merissa Jaye
Priyanka Padidam

Ruth

By Elana DeSantis

May I remember your hands as hands.
Not as a genesis of melodies that spill from stringed, wooden things,
Not as a gold-sifting plate on the riverbed from horse-drawn journeys to a Great Red West,
Not as some basin for holy water from which I no longer drink.

May I see you as a breathing, pulsing thing:
She who squeezes her eyes shut as she sings,
She who covers cucumbers slices and tomato pulp in sea salt,
She who wrote me letters pressed under her t shirt in a yellow lunchbox that says “St. Louis,”
She who I loved in the way that I loved her.

May you never become a myth that grows so big inside of me
that I must break you into paragraphs
to stomach our memory.

Elegy for Maui

By Asia Acevedo

I. Honokōwai Beach Park

the reefs span the length
of the shore, maybe farther,
in three jagged rows
keeping the sea separate
from those who ruin it.

i stand with arms unfurled
and imagine them stretching
long enough to wrap
around the decaying coral,
embracing it in apology

but i am six,
and the reefs ancient
lay still, stuck in their distrust
of two legged creatures
who desire to devastate.

i hope one day i too
will possess the secret strength
sequestered in the coral’s bleached bones
born from protecting
whom she loves most.

II. Banyan Tree Court Park
a mother spider flipped
on her back
reaches with legs unfurled
to the sky. she never reorients.

silk falls to the earth,
hangs from now still limbs,
tangles in grotesque ropes
calcifies, anchors

dust, dirt, ash
filter the light above her
softening the air
for those seeking her shade.

no one will remember
how she once towered
and with limbs restrained,
soon she too will forget.

Place of birth

No. Mariana Is., U.S.A.

By May Hong

The Mariana Trench is the deepest region of all the world’s oceans. Wikipedia says it’s “a crescent-shaped scar in the Earth's crust.” The last trickle of sunlight disappears about a tenth of the way down, the rest is total darkness. Only two manned descents have ever been made to Challenger Deep, one of which was by James Cameron, who described the floor to be “very lunar.”

In sixth grade art class, we learned how to carve linoleum blocks for printing. The teacher said to carve away from ourselves, but I still nicked my fingers so many times that she ended up just placing a box of Band-Aids next to my station. Wikipedia says a gouge is a chisel “in which the blade is curved along its length, to allow working in a hollow not otherwise accessible with a straight blade.” Making all these little u-shaped dents of investigation.

My passport assigns “No. Mariana Is., U.S.A.” as my place of birth. I listed Saipan as my hometown on Facebook, though I left before I learned to speak. It’s pretty hard to spot on most maps. At the northern tip of the island is a place called Suicide Cliff, where an estimated 8,000 Japanese soldiers and civilians collectively leapt to their deaths in the face of imminent capture by the Americans, demonstrating unwavering allegiance to their Great Empire. Wikipedia says “a contemporary correspondent” praised this as “the finest act of the Shōwa period,” but I can’t find the source.

7TH INNING STRETCH

By Jordan Lauf

When you breach the surface, it all comes rushing in at once: the meticulous brown and green borders of the diamond, the heady scent of popcorn and beer, the calls for water and crackerjacks and the echoing chatter. I always bask in the entrance for a minute, taking in air after being underwater for so long.

Baseball is a bit like church. The more I learn about it, the less it makes sense. How a person connects bat to blurred ball, how someone else catches that ball. How a pitcher can paint the outside corner just so, as if they were licking a bit of jam off the tip of their upper lip. I think that’s why my love for baseball has always been worshipful, complete, full enough to roll around in my mouth and never dissolve.

My father taught me the sacraments. The Cracker-Jack communion, the 9th inning Hail Mary, the ballgame hymns. When we perform these rituals, side by side, we almost look the same. Our actions mirror in a way our conversations never do, the ones that begin at such different angles that the lines never intersect. But when you’re at a baseball game, all that exists in the world is the delicious moment of stillness before a pitcher releases his slider or his curveball. A purposeful silence.

But games only last nine innings, or ten, or fourteen, and at the end someone has to win or lose. All religions are like that. And at the end of the game, the traffic and the grit and the sun and talk radio and fathers who don’t know you come flooding back in.

UNRAVELING

By Maya Peace

She handed me a pair of knitting needles,

some blue yarn, across the gap between our chairs. Like a peace offering. I was not of the impression we were in need of peace. I liked doing things with my hands (especially during those sessions) and so I, quietly, picked up the needles my therapist passed me, the yarn. Blue, and darker blue for the edge. We were making hats.

Knitting followed sand tray. Sand tray followed The Thoughts. The Thoughts had followed...I don’t know what exactly. But maybe we can’t remember our history, can’t trace its happenings, the ways it loops back, folds over generations and decades and moments and bodies, at 12 years old.

“You were pretty old,” my mom says in discussions we have now. And “what do you think that was about?”

I don’t know what it was about. Well I do, but not why they came. The Thoughts. They happened slowly, a subtle and quiet silky surge. Until suddenly (it felt like) my mind was screaming all the

time. Thought winds tearing apart the careful, tidy inside I constructed my head to be. (I know now it was not of a sudden. I can see the inklings, the building, the rupture in earlier memories, the short gasps, the shadows, the cruel dances in my peripheral vision hinting at the main act).

My mother as a container for the toxicity in my head: I think now of the way we dispose of nuclear waste. How we put it in strong (but not impenetrable) vessels. Hope they don’t

explode. Dump it into the sea. Pray for animals to stay unglowing. I think now of the weight my mother carried as I wept my mind into hers. I would call her, when she was far away from me. I would follow her around, whispering these destructive nothings in her ear. These nothings (in another mind, might they have been truly nothings?) that pulled me away from myself, that festered within me until I was so infected I thought I would burst. Talking to my mother as draining my own wound. Gauze arms seeping up my oozing.

...

We cast on. Perhaps 45 stitches. I don’t remember now; the patterns have faded into faintness. [knit 2 purl 2, knit 2 purl 2] I wondered if this is what everyone’s therapy sessions were like. Playing in sandboxes with small figurines. Crafting. I buried my answers in my loops of yarn, tucking them away so they didn’t escape to torment me. “I don’t knows” and “I do knows” found their way into blue and blue fibers, weaving something to keep me warm. In 50 minute chunks. “That’s our time” coming before answers. Weaving terminated. Venture forth into the cold with hat unfinished.

Venture forth to my mother as a tree, absorbing the putrid mindmatter I fed to her, filtering, offering oxygen, release from the choking. Rings of scars and stories. The lean years and bountiful marked on her mind/body. Traces of salty grief in her acorns. My mother as breath. As nourishment.

My mother cleaned out the closet of my room at home recently. She tells me that she threw out a lot of my old ballet stuff, saving only a pair of pointe shoes, a leotard. I wonder if she saw the half-finished hat in my basket of tangled yarn. If I’ve forgotten that I’ve already unraveled it. If maybe I turned that blue into another creation.

An Ode to Tropical Sno

By Ailie Orzak

I swore I would never return, yet there I was, beginning summer just like the last: turquoise walls, a neon yellow t-shirt, rainbow bottles. Kid-friendly hues spilled across the floor until they dripped down, down to the underneath and that’s where I saw it — a hidden ugly, dust and mold and shoes sticking on the melt. Blue walls pressed in on me so I could no longer breathe, and my throat closed around vials of sugary syrup, choking on the overly sweet promise of new things to come.

And the snow cones weren’t as sweet as they were the summer before. They coated my teeth and tongue, twisted my stomach with their fake flavor. So I didn’t eat them. I didn’t eat anything, really.

At first the shop filled with sound. My friends brightened the colors with their company and laughed with me during work’s in-betweens. He was there too, crowding the window bench with them till closing time when we would head out into the summer evening that was made for us — because this was the beginning of the end and we weren’t ready to let go of our world, hanging on to every last bite of our snow cones.

And things were good. But I still didn’t eat the cones, and my throat still closed.

The season went on and I began to hide alone in the shop until it went so quiet that the colors consumed me as I stepped on sugar spills, sneakers sticking on the syrup that smeared the floor. Outside of work, I still owned the nights with my friends. We would run towards the ends of our small town and beyond to the city where we bought snow cones from somewhere else, laughing at how much better they were as we sat on the edge of the Lake Michigan and watched the skyline glow. Yet even in those brighter moments, I was still stuck and slipping and unable to stop the ugly of the store from seeping in through my shoes. So I didn’t let him come around much anymore. I didn’t want him to see me dissolving.

The summer expired, breaking down into even more dust and mold while the shop walls pressed in tighter around me till the night he and I left the store for the last time. For the next few shifts my only hope was that the customers couldn’t tell that in my eyes, the blues and the yellows and the rainbows of the shop were a blur that liquidized and leaked down my cheeks, filling up my throat so that it tightened more than ever in order to keep myself from spilling over.

But through it all, in they came. Friends filed in to grab my syrup-stained hands and pull me from the underneath. We danced together while the final drops of the summer’s cones melted, covering every inch of our town as we melted too, dripping up, up, and away along with our world.

And so the summer departed, whisking me away with it. Miles from that store, it took many months for me to learn to eat again and much longer for my throat to loosen wide enough to let the present and future in. I’ve pushed the shop far away from me, yet the summer’s sugary traces still linger, the final remnants sticking to my skin.

Even now, I’m slowly scrubbing the last bits of syrup from my fingers.

i slice into an orange

the early season, a fleshy fall day

flourescent juice runs down the side of the counter

my hangnails will sting

The Chains We Don’t See

By Austin Towns

Lately I've been trying to cage my mind to the present. But it seems like if my mind wanders free it always finds itself in the past.

My last girlfriend used to call me a loser to show her affection. As if she was trying to apologize or seem like she understood a plight. Being black. Being a loser. Somehow these two concepts were intertwined. Like I was a loser the moment I was born into this world with dark skin. Disadvantaged. Behind. It was not a choice. It was a birthright.

Entire generations of brothers sisters mothers fathers sons daughters forced into mental illness in the fields and the houses. Uncles become fathers. Grandmothers forced to rear a child again with their outdated knowledge about the world. How much learning the father didn’t pass down to his son or daughter because he was displaced on another plantation or he died in the streets of South Central Los Angeles or he martyred himself in his office, day in day out, because he believed his co-workers were light years ahead of him, because he believed he had this chronic illness called being black in America.

Not just driving, living. Living while black is a state of perpetual anxiety. Wondering how one day I’ll have to explain to my children that they were born into this world with a blessing and a curse. Explain to them that they will be subjugated to a racism I probably won't understand, because it is a racism so subtle, the only indicator of its existence will be in the tears from my children's faces.

And now I look up. And I walk. And I live. A young black man trying to find his way. And I ask myself when the day will come when I will see past racism, depression, and self-pity. I ask myself when I will step into God's glory and see that this skin, this struggle is a blessing. This melanin is a cure. This life is a happy one.

Diaspora Daughter Blues

By Muna Mohamed

my first bad word was ceeb. it means private. ceeb was the response to every time my body would talk. ceeb was my body. my body was my first bad word. ceeb is the only way i know how to talk to my body. i hold my body in private, and ceeb is what happens when i try to break out.

ceeb is a jail cell that parts of me have always been locked in. i entered this cell at age 9, when i first realized my body was different. a woman is a joke, and her body is the punchline. i remember the first joke made about my body. my mother looked over at my aunt, and asked, have we checked her halaalays recently? halal is the word they use to describe what is permissible, like meat. they chuckled, a laughter drenched in pain, or conformity. i can’t tell the difference. when they cut parts of me i became halal. became permissible. became every man’s dream. did you know that you are a fantasy? how dare we leave you open?

it’s funny - how my body became a public matter the day they called me gabar. how they make you think your body was born in private. a secret. it’s no wonder we’ve always been good at keeping those. my body is a secret i keep in private.

back home is the place i am most insecure. when i went back home, i learned to be private. when i was 9, i learned my first secret. when they ask me where i’m from, i don’t know what to say. back home, they don’t talk about this, back home they don’t talk, back home, you are taught to fear yourself, to look in the mirror and never see enough. girls here cut school to go on vacation. but we go on vacation to get cut. to some, woman means meat. and what is a man but a butcher? patriarchy but a warehouse? and love something i search for in every man’s chest.

my home country cut me, and now, hurt is my mother tongue.

i call this diaspora daughter blues, a girl simply looking for a place to call home. a place that will hold her, caress her, kiss her on the cheek, tell her she is whole, even if parts of her are missing. a place that will tell her the truth, what happened to her, who did this to her, and why.

ceeb: somali word meaning private, usually in reference to woman’s body parts, sex, etc.

gabar: somali word meaning girl

A Tendency to Ask

By Ethan Laurence Resik

You find yourself between a bus seat
and a therapist’s couch.
A chameleon of both surfaces,
you find a universality in
providing imitations of what you see:

mirrored photographs of Narcissus.
This is a bad night
of your own prismed creation.
Passersby call it completely normal.

Through the smoke and incense, a dullness
vibrates. Your warm-blooded body pulses from
heart to tapping feet; he speaks to you,
“you know, I didn’t really get it but I still love it.”
Wishing you got it, you smile. Such a true smile.

Yet you fear a good night for what
proceeds. Every human knows that feeling,
I think. You walk from the stairs, outside,
and look up. You see the uncontrollable
starlight reflect on your chest.
Footsteps approach somewhere nearby.

Reinvent

By Emmett Pinsky

because of their physical state, it might seem wrong to speak of cracking open clouds.
vapor is not brittle but it has come to my attention that
even sweetness does not preclude the eventuality of this nasty type of snap.

ever-wondering my metadiegetic status as an object in your world.
how much narrative importance do I get?
someone said this, once:
“one slow-motion sequence of fluttering birds in a slow-motion film makes for a potent
metadiegetic signifier; one hundred slow-motion sequences of fluttering birds makes for a nature documentary.”

i might be so boring as to say:
i do my best writing in airports.

now that the clouds have cracked open, our hands have become tools
in the way we have been told they once were, before we invented
shoplifting, fonts, I.O.U.s, mothers, montages, bookmarks, breakables, screenshots,
and therapy.

don’t reinvent the wheel, they say.
but if there is joy in the invention?
cycles of creation give life give light!
please please please do not fault me for tilting my head toward that ceaseless becoming.

perhaps that is why Carson called them the Cycladic people.
inventing and reinventing the purse, grindstones, and insomnia.

long ago, they told us, our hands were tools
and with them we clapped and scratched and held.

the clouds cracked open and we dropped to the ground, hands doing exactly what they are supposed to
(catching us as we land, land.)

we are left alone with only our hands to gather the time that has been so spoiled.
eventually we will invent the Jar, which holds time better than hands,
then the shelf, which holds jars better than sand.

given its physical state, it might seem wrong to speak of time reflecting light
but given recent events i feel confident in saying that
the best clocks speak through sun
and look over shoulders toward
some other becoming

Liminal

By Grace Konstantin

I. the walk


tiles in the morning are slick
with dew or washing
and our heels click in time with the metal kegs
cheers-ing their rims while collected
in the early mist from the front walk
of a pub


we are quiet, we shiver for the sun
has not yet warmed the sunken air
plumes are expelled from between lips or nostrils
with hands shoved deep deep
into the confidence of pockets
we march on

II. the clouds


a fog closes in on us from all sides
and it feels like the only thing
here
is our little stretch of road
beneath a big blue bus

III. the self


I am so many patterns of blue
the sky wraps around my ankles and
tucks into my rubber boots
a speckled umbrella swings
from two fingers by my knees
the rain tickles the tops of my cheeks


I am safe in oversized plaid rolled
to the elbows and the leaves
look up as I pass, splayed
on their backs
upon pooling wrinkled pavement


I forget where I am going

Retail

By Priyanka Padidam

Before the storm I’m staticked in the canned foods aisle, wondering if Cheez Whiz is actually good when a man reminds me that men can see me. I don’t buy it.

That winter was half intake forms and half nodding to advice from wayward older women like,
70% of your body should be vegetables. Everything in or on your body should probably be brand
name. Except for pregnancy tests, those don’t matter.

Sunday finds me couch-crusted in front of a dude in a docuseries on the 1980s saying
Life was good! There were products to buy!

You know some people in El Paso go to Mexican pharmacies just to ask for over-the-counter birth control. Bordered, they cross and buy it.

Tonight: a train-dream turned plane-crash. The metal pill hardwares towards the ground and a stranger turns to me and smiles because it’s the last thing to give.

Old Joke

By Anonymous

I can’t get it out of my head, the Doritos truck
behind the hearse, not part of any procession, but still. God

I am sorry for laughing at that, this too:
the woman taking photos of the open casket at

my distant uncle’s too early funeral while my grandma
by proxy tries to set me up with a third cousin—

he’s so handsome, you know, and smart and you two
would just hit it off—and right then I could have died

of embarrassment. at Queer
death, my relatives
sad react to Newtown not Orlando. The little cousins sharing

repost if you hate cancer, 88% of you won’t follow
that old joke: only the bagpiper rejoices at the death

of a firefighter, in his best kilt on the rotary
during rush hour. the widow’s grief thinly veiled

driving home, I hear that mournful wail but my god
you wouldn’t believe the traffic
